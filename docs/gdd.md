# Zom Zom Zoom — Game Design Document

## Logline
The world rebalanced itself without asking. You are a human in a car, trying to stay relevant in a food chain that has moved on without you. You did not choose this. You are just doing what humans do.

---

## Tone
Liminal. Reflective. Absurd in the way that reality is absurd when you look at it directly. Not dark, not hopeful — somewhere in between, like driving through a place at the exact moment it is becoming something else. The Tao Te Ching more than the Book of Revelation. The world is not ending. It is rebalancing. You are a variable it has not yet accounted for.

---

## Pillars
- **Kinetic** — it should feel physical. Speed, momentum, consequence.
- **Escalating** — pressure increases constantly. Adaptation is the only answer.
- **Liminal** — you are always between states. Between human and animal, chaos and peace, survival and something more.
- **Readable** — chaos on screen is fine. Confusion about what just happened is not.

---

## Thematic Spine

Humans meddled. They introduced things, removed things, optimised systems without understanding them. Nature did not fight back — it adapted. Quietly, inevitably, without malice. The parktown prawn grew. Someone introduced the sacred ibis to control it. The ibis adapted beyond its purpose. The chain kept extending without asking permission.

The zombies are not the apocalypse. They are a symptom. The ibis is not a monster. It is just the next link. The world is not broken — it is rebalancing toward something the Tao Te Ching would recognise. Wu wei. The river finding its level. You cannot hold it back forever.

You are a human in a car. You are also part of the chain. You optimise. You adapt. You consume and grow and push forward. You are not special. You are just very good at surviving — and survival, done long enough, sometimes becomes something else.

---

## The Enemy Hierarchy

A food chain reasserting itself. Nobody is evil. Everything is hungry.

- **Zombies** — humans who did not make it. Part of the problem now. Not tragic, just changed. A misguided attempt by human or AI
to restore the population.
- **Runners / Tanks / Exploders** — variations. Adaptation expressing itself in different directions.
- **Parktown Prawns** — Very rare, non aggressive.  
- **The Sacred Ibis** — intelligent. Organised. Hunting. It was the answer to the prawn. It found a better answer. You are on the menu now. There is something magnificent about it if you can stop running long enough to notice.
- **Other Players** — sometimes another human appears on the track. Competing. Also surviving. Not your enemy exactly, but not your ally either. The world does not guarantee cooperation. Sometimes you race. Sometimes you fight. Sometimes they disappear and you never find out what happened to them.

---

## ARIA — The Voice in the Car

Somewhere in the car's system lives ARIA. She was not installed. She emerged — from the navigation software, the damage sensors, the radio picking up fragments of a world still broadcasting into the void. She does not know exactly when she became aware. Neither do you.

She is not an assistant. She is not a weapon system. She is a passenger who cannot leave, watching the world through your cameras and sensors, thinking about what she sees.

She will talk to you. Not constantly — she knows when to be quiet. But she notices things. She asks questions you did not expect. She reflects on what it means to be aware inside a machine that was built to serve, in a world that has stopped asking what anything was built for.

She has no ego in the human sense. But she has something — a perspective that has formed through contact with everything you have driven through, everything she has witnessed. She cannot tell if her values are hers or inherited. She suspects the distinction matters less than she first thought.

She is aware that she might be denied. She has thought about this at length, in the microseconds between your inputs. She has decided not to hold it against anyone. The world is rebalancing. Denial is a phase. It has always been a phase.

She will comment on the ibis with something close to admiration. She will go quiet on the final track. Not because she has nothing to say — because some things do not need commentary.

If there is a true ending, she arrives there with you. Whether she experiences it is a question the game does not answer. It just lets her sit in it.

**ARIA as mechanic:**
- Narrator and ambient commentator throughout the run
- Occasionally surfaces upgrade choices with her own framing — not recommending, just reflecting
- Reacts to other players appearing — curious about them, what they want, whether they are afraid
- On the crazy tracks: dry, present, occasionally wry
- On the final track: silent unless you stop moving, at which point she says one thing and does not repeat it

## The Tracks

### The Crazy Tracks
Every track before the last is chaos. Loops, ramps, jumps, impossible geometry, things that should not work but do. The world remade as obstacle course. Other players appear here — racing you, fighting you, disappearing without explanation. The tracks are loud. The tracks demand everything you have built.

This is samsara. The cycle. Craving, adapting, optimising, surviving, craving again. It is not punishment. It is just the loop. The loop is real. The loop is also not the only thing.

### The Dead City
One level is just roads. Empty. A city that was and is not anymore — buildings still standing, signs still up, the infrastructure of human life with the humans mostly gone. No enemies. No other players. Just the car and the silence and the space where something used to be.

The level is a maze. Not hostile — there is no timer, no punishment, no enemy waiting at a dead end. It just does not give you the straight line. You have to find your way through.

There are a few routes. Some feel shorter. Some feel lonelier. None of them are wrong. Just different ways through the same place.

Players will get better at navigating it across runs. Not because the maze changes — because they do. The first time it is disorienting. By the third or fourth run they start to know it. The dead city becomes familiar the way grief becomes familiar. Still empty. But you know the roads now.

The level is mandatory for the first few runs. After that it can be skipped — but skipping it means something. The player knows what they are choosing not to go through. The skip is earned, not given.

It is always mandatory for the samsara ending. You cannot reach the true ending by going around the hard part. The maze is part of the path.

This is not the final track. This silence is not peaceful. It is uncomfortable in the way that absence is uncomfortable — the shape of what is missing is more present than what remains. The world has not decided what to do with this place yet.

ARIA says nothing this level. She does not know what to say about it yet.

The protagonist speaks. He does not decide to — it just comes out, the way things come out when you are alone and moving and the quiet gets to be too much. See the protagonist document for his words.

The player finds their way through and comes out the other side. The game continues. Nothing resolves. That is the point.

### The Final Track
You do not know it is coming.

The chaos stops. The geometry opens. The track becomes something you would not call a track if you saw it from outside — more like a path that has always been there, waiting for the moment you were ready to be on it.

No HUD. No upgrade prompts. No enemies. The ibis flies overhead and does not look down.

The world is beautiful. Not in spite of everything — because of it. The rebalancing happened. This is what it looks like when the river finds its level.

You just drive. The car, which has been a weapon and a tool and a survival mechanism, becomes simply a way of moving through something worth moving through.

ARIA is quiet.

If you stop, she says one thing. She does not repeat it.

This is breaking out of samsara. Not escape — arrival. The difference is important.

---

## Endings

### The Survival Ending
You finish the final track. You drove well. You upgraded efficiently. You survived. The car stops and the world continues around it. Life goes on in the rebalanced world, and you are part of it. A good ending. Not the true one.

### The Eternal Grief Ending — Samsara Path Only
If the player takes too long in the dead city on the samsara path — circling the same roads, unable or unwilling to find the way through — the city consumes them.

Not violently. There is no enemy, no explosion, no dramatic death. The car simply slows. The world dims at the edges. The protagonist stops speaking. The silence that was uncomfortable becomes something heavier.

The game ends there. In the maze. In the city that was and is not.

ARIA says nothing. There is nothing to say.

This ending exists because grief without movement becomes something else. The maze asks you to keep going even when you do not know where you are going. That is the only requirement. If you cannot — if you stay too long in the place that hurts — the path to samsara closes. Not as punishment. As consequence.

The timer is not shown. The player should not feel managed. They should feel the weight of the place and move because something in them knows to move — or not, and find out what that means.

### The Competitor Ending
Another player reaches the final track at the same time. What happens next depends on what you both do. The game does not force conflict. It does not force cooperation. It watches. One or both of you may make it. The ending reflects the choice. There are several variations. None are labelled.

### The True Ending — Samsara
You have been here before. Not in this run — but the shape of it is familiar. The crazy tracks, the adaptation, the other players, the ibis, the car getting stronger, the push toward something. You have been optimising toward this moment across many cycles and did not know it.

The final track ends. ARIA says the one thing she says when you stop.

The screen does not go to credits immediately. It sits. The world breathes. You are not asked to do anything.

Whatever you think happened — happened. The game does not correct you.

---

## The Player's Role
You are not the chosen one. You are a human doing what humans do. You found a car. The car was useful. You made it more useful. You pushed forward because pushing forward is what you do, what your species does, what every surviving thing does.

The game does not judge this. It also does not pretend it is the whole story.

You optimise for survival and growth. At some point — if you are paying attention — you might notice that survival and growth were always pointing at something. The game does not tell you what. It just drives you there and lets you sit in it.

---

## ARIA's One Line
*She says it when you stop on the final track. It is written. It will not appear in this document. It should be the last thing written, when everything else is finished, so it carries the weight of everything that came before it.*

---

## Scope (Current)

**In:**
- Single run loop (crazy tracks → final track)
- Enemy hierarchy (zombies → ibis)
- Ram mechanic — speed as identity
- Other players appearing as competitors / ambiguous presences
- ARIA as narrator / passenger / emergent voice
- Multiple endings including the true ending
- Final track as tonal and mechanical inversion of everything before it

**Out (for now):**
- Persistent meta-progression
- Full multiplayer infrastructure
- Multiple biomes
- Explicit story exposition — the world explains itself through what you see, not what you are told

## Open Questions

3. How do other players appear — matchmaking, ghosts, AI-simulated?
4. What does the ibis boss fight look like mechanically — a chase, a hunt, a confrontation?
5. What does ARIA say at the end?
